Short-term Rental Regulations Impact Analysis (2010-2024)

This internal presentation was prepared by Scott Glasgow from FinMango for Chen Zhang, PhD from Iowa State University to help analyze Google search trends related to short-term rental regulations, housing affordability, and labor markets over a 15-year period.

Understanding the Data

The data used in this analysis represents Google search query volumes for various terms related to short-term rentals from 2010 to 2024. Here's what you need to know:

  • Data Source: Google Trends API.
  • Data Format: The values represent the probability of a search term multiplied by 10 million.
  • Interpretation: Higher values indicate greater search interest for a particular term relative to other terms in the dataset. A value of 1,000,000 in the dataset represents a 10% probability or rate. For example, if the "Affordable Housing" search query value is 1,317,475, it means there's approximately a 13.17% rate of searches.
  • Time Range: The data covers the period from 2010 to 2024, allowing us to observe long-term trends in public interest regarding these topics.

Search Interest: Housing Affordability and Regulations

This chart shows the trends in search interest for affordable housing, short-term rental laws, and Airbnb regulations from 2010 to 2024. Key observations:

  • Search interest in affordable housing has generally increased over the 15-year period, indicating growing public concern or awareness.
  • Interest in short-term rental laws and Airbnb regulations began to appear in searches around 2015, with significant increases after 2020.
  • The rise in searches for regulatory terms may reflect increasing public awareness of policy changes or debates surrounding short-term rentals.
  • Note the different scales: Affordable housing searches are much more frequent than regulation-related searches.

Search Interest: Labor Market and Regulations

This chart illustrates the trends in search interest for hospitality jobs, cleaning services, and short-term rental regulations from 2010 to 2024. Key observations:

  • Search interest in cleaning services has shown a general increase over the 15-year period, potentially indicating growing demand or awareness of these services.
  • Interest in hospitality jobs has fluctuated, with a notable spike in 2020 followed by a sharp decline, possibly reflecting job market changes during the COVID-19 pandemic.
  • The emergence of searches for short-term rental laws and Airbnb regulations coincides with changes in search patterns for hospitality jobs and cleaning services, particularly after 2015.
  • Again, note the different scales: Cleaning services and hospitality jobs have much higher search volumes than the regulation-related terms.

Correlation Analysis of Search Interest (2010-2024)

This table shows the correlations between search interests for various terms related to short-term rental regulations, housing affordability, and labor market indicators over the 15-year period from 2010 to 2024. Key points to consider:

  • The correlation between search interests for short-term rental laws/Airbnb regulations and affordable housing may indicate how public attention to these topics has evolved together over time.
  • The relationship between regulation-related searches and hospitality jobs searches might reflect public awareness of how these regulations could impact the job market.
  • Correlations with cleaning services searches could suggest changing perceptions about the short-term rental industry's impact on related services.
  • These correlations are based on search interest data from Google Trends, where values represent probabilities multiplied by 10 million. They reflect public attention and concerns, not necessarily real-world conditions or policy effectiveness.
  • The differing scales of search volumes (e.g., affordable housing having much higher volumes than regulation terms) may influence correlation results and should be considered in interpretation.

Note: Correlation values range from -1 (perfect negative correlation) to 1 (perfect positive correlation). 0 indicates no correlation.

Strength guide:

  • 0.7 to 1.0 (or -0.7 to -1.0): Strong correlation
  • 0.5 to 0.7 (or -0.5 to -0.7): Moderate correlation
  • 0.3 to 0.5 (or -0.3 to -0.5): Weak correlation
  • 0.0 to 0.3 (or 0.0 to -0.3): Very weak correlation
